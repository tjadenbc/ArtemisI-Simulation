The question

Shouldn't modern be dramatically higher?

Native headlines: Apollo 11 (crewed, full landing) 88.00% · Artemis I (uncrewed, no landing) 93.00%.

Side by side, the naive expectation is that 57 years of technology should make the modern number dramatically higher. It's about five points higher — and even that edge turns out to be an artifact of accounting, not of hardware. The two headlines measure different missions, with different success definitions, over different exposure times: Apollo's 88% includes a lunar landing (its riskiest, most crew-exposed phase); Artemis never lands. Pull those confounds apart and the honest comparison emerges — and it lands on parity. Getting there first required correcting a same-class modelling artifact on both sides.

Ground rules · shared methodology

What is held fixed, and what is allowed to differ

Era-honest, not normalized. Each mission keeps its NATIVE success definition — Apollo gates on CREW SURVIVAL (1969 could not fly or return without a live crew; crew loss = mission loss); Artemis I gates on VEHICLE INTACT (it flew the entire profile autonomously). The autonomy asymmetry is a real 2022 capability, so it is not averaged away. Rates stay era-appropriate (that difference IS the measurement). Time-driven hazards use one shared Poisson exposure law so a 25-day mission is compared to an 8-day one on equal footing. Natural-environment fluxes (SPE, micrometeoroid) share one occurrence λ; per-vehicle shielding is applied separately.

Why not higher — the decomposition

Where the era difference actually lives

Parity, not a modern blowout, because the 2022 advantages and the 2022 burdens roughly cancel on this profile:

Era factors that LOWER the 2022 number

3× exposure — 25.45 d vs ~8.2 d; the time-driven hazards (systems, MMOD, thermal, SPE) accrue over roughly three times longer (~1.9 pp, analytic; 1.93 pp re-derived on the final run). First-flight rates — Artemis I's failure probabilities are estimate-grade (heritage + PRA; no flown base), against Apollo's flown-CSM record. Harder profile — a 25-day distant retrograde orbit plus an extended-skip entry, versus a short lunar-orbit-and-return; genuinely more places to fail.

Era factors that RAISE the 2022 number

Modern components — redundant avionics, an ATV-derived service module, better propulsion reliability. Full autonomy — Artemis carries no crew to lose. But on a NO-LANDING profile that advantage is ≈ 0: with no surface operations there are essentially no crew-only-fatal modes, so "no crew to lose" removes almost nothing. The autonomy premium is a LANDING-mission phenomenon — Apollo's own crew−vehicle gap is +2.0 pp only on its landing profile (crew 94.00% vs vehicle 96.02%), and ≈ 0 on no-landing (crew 94.28% vs vehicle 94.21%).

Net: the burdens and the advantages land within noise of each other, and the vehicle metrics tie. The honest headline is "1969-flown-hardware on a short simple profile ≈ 2022-first-flight-hardware on a long hard profile" — modernity buys its edge exactly where the mission lets it (autonomy, on a crewed landing), and spends it back on exposure and profile difficulty.

Honest caveats

What this comparison is and isn't

◇All Artemis rates are estimate-grade. Two flown Artemis missions, both successful — no statistical failure base. Inputs are heritage reliabilities + NASA PRA targets + foreseeable-problem analysis. The number is only as good as those.
◇Different profiles, deliberately. Each mission flies its REAL trajectory (Apollo low-lunar-orbit, Artemis distant retrograde orbit). The comparison matches on metric + no-landing scope, not on identical flight paths — that difference is part of the era story, not an error.
◇Parity is a band overlap, not a tie to the decimal. Artemis 93.00–94.29% vs Apollo 94.21% (the Apollo vehicle-only figure predates the Apollo model's July 2026 recalibration (documented in the Apollo repository); its measured effect on the strictly-more-exposed landing configuration was −0.08 pp, within sampling) (CI 93.74–94.65) with overlapping CIs; the claim is statistical indistinguishability on the transport-leg vehicle metric, not that one equals the other exactly.
◇Two independent codebases, shared nature. Gravity / ephemeris / time / atmosphere models are common infrastructure; mission profile, vehicle config, rates, and success definition are per-mission and never cross-contaminate.
